Festivals and Events



Independence Day


Sri Lankan Independence Day, celebrated on February 4th, is a nationwide event filled with grand festivities. The capital city of Colombo hosts a magnificent military parade, accompanied by political speeches and ceremonies. Cultural programs highlighting Sri Lankan music, dance, food, and history add to the festive atmosphere. Regional pageants and celebrations take place across the country, ensuring that the spirit of independence is experienced by all. Visitors in Colombo can witness the military parade, airshows, fireworks, the iconic flag-hoisting ceremony by the President, and other traditional elements such as the ceremonial double-ended drums (magul bera) and the chanting of the Jayamangala Gatha.

Formerly known as Ceylon, Sri Lanka played a significant role as an economic hub and port for the British Empire. It gained semi-independent 'dominion' status in 1948 and became a full republic on May 22, 1972, adopting the name the Democratic Socialist Republic of Sri Lanka. Don Stephen Senanayake, the first Prime Minister, is revered as the Father of the Nation, and William Gopallawa became the first President, replacing the British Empire's Governor.

Independence Day holds great importance and is considered a major national holiday. As a result, bars, clubs, and liquor stores remain closed. Public institutions such as banks and post offices also do not operate on this day, so it is advisable to plan any official tasks accordingly.

Sinhala and Tamil New Year


While many countries celebrate the new year on January 1st, Sri Lanka follows a different calendar and celebrates the Sinhala and Tamil New Year on April 13th and 14th. This is a significant occasion in Sri Lanka, filled with rich rituals and customs, and is a must-experience for anyone visiting the island. The new year celebration is based on the movement of the sun from the House of Pieces to the House of Aries. It involves lively activities such as firecrackers, fireworks, and enjoying traditional sweets.

Also known as the sun festival, the Sinhala and Tamil new year has been practiced for centuries as a way to pay homage to the God of Sun. This festival provides a unique opportunity to witness Sri Lanka's core traditions, rituals, and participate in fun activities and traditional games that are exclusive to this event.

One of the significant rituals during the new year is the neutral period, also known as Nonagathe, which begins on the 13th. During this time, people refrain from work and engage in religious activities to seek blessings and prepare for the new year. This period holds great importance in the overall celebration.

Vesak


Vesak is a significant religious and cultural festival observed in Sri Lanka, typically taking place on the day of the full moon in May. This festival holds great importance for Buddhists around the world, as it commemorates important events from the life of Lord Buddha.

Vesak Day marks the birth of Siddhartha Gautama, who later became known as Lord Buddha. According to tradition, he was born under a Sat tree in Lumbini, Nepal. The second significant event celebrated on Vesak is Siddhartha Gautama's attainment of enlightenment, becoming the Buddha, the Enlightened One. The third event remembered on this day is the Parinirvana, the passing away of Lord Buddha over 2500 years ago in Kusinagar.

Besides Sri Lanka, Vesak is celebrated in various Asian countries, including India, Japan, Singapore, and Taiwan. In Sri Lanka, the festival is accompanied by a range of religious activities. These include Sil campaigns, where devotees observe moral precepts, Bodhi Poojas, offering devotions to the sacred Bodhi tree, Dansalas, which are food distribution centers providing free meals, and the creation of pandols (thoran) and lanterns. Additionally, Vesak devotional songs, known as Bakthi Gee, are also performed during this period.

Esala Perahara


The Esala Perahera in Kandy is a renowned Buddhist festival in Sri Lanka, celebrated in the month of Esala to commemorate the Buddha's first teaching after enlightenment. Spanning ten days, the festival features a captivating procession called the Perahera, showcasing dancers, musicians, fire-breathers, and lavishly decorated elephants. The highlight of the festival is the sacred tooth relic of the Buddha, which is paraded through the streets of Kandy with great reverence and panache. The Perahera is considered one of the most beautiful pageants in Asia and attracts thousands of spectators.

The festival begins with the ritual of "Kap Situweema," where a sanctified young Jackfruit Tree is planted at an auspicious time. The tree is adorned with sandalwood-scented water, nine different types of flowers, and an oil lamp with nine wicks. Prayers are offered by the priest of the Maha Vishnu Devale to invoke blessings from the gods. This ritual sets the stage for the commencement of the festival and signifies its spiritual significance.

The Esala Perahera is a cherished event that showcases the rich cultural heritage and religious devotion of Sri Lanka. It is a grand celebration filled with vibrant performances and the symbolic representation of the sacred tooth relic. The festival brings together locals and visitors in a joyous atmosphere, where they can witness the captivating procession, immerse themselves in the traditions of the country, and experience the beauty and splendor of the Esala Perahera firsthand.

Sripada Pilgrimage


The Sri Pada pilgrimage is a seasonal event that occurs from December to May annually, attracting numerous pilgrims who gather to pay their respects to the sacred footprint atop Adam's Peak. Located in Ratnapura, the city of gems, this majestic mountain rises 7,000 feet above sea level. It is a pilgrimage that unites followers of the four major religions in Sri Lanka.

According to Buddhist beliefs, the sacred footprint on the peak belongs to Buddha, who visited the mountain at the invitation of its guardian deity. Hindus consider it to be the footprint of Shiva, the revered Hindu God of Destruction. Christians believe it belongs to St. Thomas, and for Muslims, it is associated with Adam, who sought refuge on the mountain after being expelled from the Garden of Eden.

During the Sri Pada pilgrimage, participants follow various traditional customs. Along the ascent, pilgrims pause to throw a threaded needle into the bushes, symbolizing the legend of Lord Buddha mending his robe during his climb. At the Seetha Gangula, a stream halfway to the summit, pilgrims cleanse themselves with the icy water, which rejuvenates them for the final stretch. It is considered inappropriate to ask about the remaining journey time, so instead, pilgrims greet each other with the phrase "Karunavai" or "Peace." Many recite religious poems to inspire and strengthen themselves during the challenging ascent.

Sri Lanka, often referred to as the "Pearl of the Indian Ocean," is a captivating destination known for its stunning natural beauty, rich cultural heritage, and warm hospitality. From pristine beaches and lush tea plantations to ancient temples and wildlife-filled national parks, Sri Lanka offers a diverse range of experiences for travelers.
